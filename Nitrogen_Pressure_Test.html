Reference — Gay-Lussac's Law & Pressure Testing

Gay-Lussac's Law (constant volume)
P1 / T1 = P2 / T2
Pressure and temperature are directly proportional at constant volume.
MUST use absolute pressure (PSIA) and absolute temperature (Rankine).
Conversions
PSIA = PSIG + 14.7
°R = °F + 459.67
Rankine is the absolute temperature scale for Fahrenheit.
0°R = absolute zero = −459.67°F
Solving for P2
P2 = P1 × (T2 / T1)
All values must be absolute (PSIA and °R).
Convert back: PSIG = PSIA − 14.7

Worked Example

You pressurize to 150 PSIG at 90°F. Overnight the temperature drops to 55°F. What should the gauge read?

Step 1: Convert to absolute
P1 = 150 + 14.7 = 164.7 PSIA
T1 = 90 + 459.67 = 549.67°R
T2 = 55 + 459.67 = 514.67°R

Step 2: Apply Gay-Lussac's Law
P2 = 164.7 × (514.67 / 549.67) = 164.7 × 0.9363 = 154.2 PSIA

Step 3: Convert back to gauge
P2 = 154.2 − 14.7 = 139.5 PSIG

Result: A 35°F temperature drop causes the gauge to read about 10.5 PSI lower. This is NOT a leak — it's physics!

Pressure Test Procedures

Typical N2 test pressure (ACR copper)150-300 PSIG
Typical N2 test pressure (refrigeration)150-500 PSIG
Standing test duration24 hours minimum
Acceptable pressure loss0 PSIG (after temp compensation)
Always use a regulatorN2 tanks are 2,000+ PSI!
Never use oxygenExplosion risk with oil!
Procedure: 1) Pressurize with dry nitrogen through a regulator. 2) Record pressure and ambient temperature. 3) Wait 24 hours. 4) Record final pressure and temperature. 5) Calculate expected pressure using Gay-Lussac's Law. 6) Compare — any difference beyond temp compensation = leak. 7) If leak found, use soap bubbles or electronic detector to locate.

Rule of Thumb

At 150 PSIG and typical ambient temperatures, every 1°F change causes roughly 0.3 PSIG change in the same direction. This is approximate — use the calculator for precision. At higher test pressures, the change per degree is larger.
